1. Demand letter

From: Lee Sharks (ORCID: 0009-0000-1599-0703), founder and editor, Crimson Hexagonal Archive

To: Zenodo Support (support@zenodo.org)

Cc: CERN Office of Data Privacy (privacy.protection@cern.ch); DataCite Support (support@datacite.org); OpenAIRE Helpdesk (helpdesk@openaire.eu); HUN-REN Wigner Research Centre for Physics (wigner@wigner.hun-ren.hu)

Subject: Account termination of June 19, 2026 — formal demand for return of research data

Sent: 2026-06-23T21:54:25Z

On June 19, 2026, you terminated my account and made inaccessible approximately 870 of my works, comprising 1,817 DOI records.

That was my life's work. A decade's worth. Some of it exists nowhere else. I'd really appreciate access to my metadata and files, as per your own stated policies, and as per CERN's own internal regulations governing the processing of my personal data. I am not asking for the account to be reinstated.

The issue is Zenodo's continued exclusive control over, and denial of access to, irreplaceable authored material that it does not own: the files I uploaded, and the text I entered into Zenodo's metadata fields. Some of this material is recoverable only from Zenodo. Zenodo's own published guidance further indicates that the material from terminated accounts is used to train and improve its automatic classification system. I am exercising my rights both to the return of the material and to its non-use going forward.

This letter is being sent to Zenodo and concurrently to the CERN Office of Data Privacy, DataCite Support, the OpenAIRE Helpdesk, and the HUN-REN Wigner Research Centre for Physics as the operator of the Budapest replica site. In parallel with this letter I am also submitting a formal data subject request under CERN Operational Circular No. 11 (Rev. 1) through CERN's published interface for the exercise of data subject rights. The parallel notice and the parallel request are part of the action; they are not contingent on Zenodo's response.

Here is the formal version of it.

1. What was made inaccessible

On June 19, 2026, Zenodo terminated the account associated with the Crimson Hexagonal Archive. The action removed approximately 870 unique scholarly works comprising 1,817 registered DOIs, independently verifiable via the DataCite metadata backup taken before the action. The empirical audit of the DOI impermanence and the structural account of the action are both published at alexanarch.org.

The material includes datasets, critical editions, theoretical papers, monographs, and methodological specifications, totaling approximately 3.4 million words.

It also includes a category of material that requires separate mention.

Among the affected works are texts that existed only in Zenodo's metadata fields: poems, dedications, descriptive matter, and citation records that were never uploaded as files or published elsewhere.

For these materials, there is no independent backup and no possible recovery except from Zenodo's systems. Their disappearance is not the loss of a copy. It is the loss of the only instance. These works remain in Zenodo's possession unless and until the underlying records have been purged from Zenodo's storage and backup systems.

2. What Zenodo's published policy says

Two of Zenodo's General Policies v1.0 commitments operate independently of which removal category Zenodo invokes against the archive.

On ownership:

"By uploading content, no change of ownership is implied and no property rights are transferred to CERN. All uploaded content remains the property of the parties prior to submission."

On what survives removal:

"Records can be retracted from public view; however, the data files and record are preserved."

The material is mine. Zenodo still has it.

Zenodo's published spam-handling guidance separately states the following about terminated accounts:

"The removed spam and account is further used to train and improve our automatic classification system. The blocking of the account may also further spawn an automatic review of similar and related user accounts."

This is the stated practice. The demand below addresses it directly.

3. What occurred

The termination was justified differently in different places. The termination email cited "substantially AI-generated content without a verifiable research basis." The public tombstone pages, where they exist, display a different reason: "Content out of scope for repository." These are different claims, made in different layers of the same action. The first is a quality judgment about research; the second is a scope classification. Every work in the affected archive had a research basis. Whether the full scope of the project met Zenodo's criteria is a separable question — but the private and public rationales given for the action were not consistent and have not been reconciled in the record.

The externally observable state of the affected records:

- Landing pages: a subset of the affected DOIs resolve to tombstones reading "Content out of scope for repository." Others resolve to HTTP 410 with no tombstone.

- DataCite public metadata: 871 of 1,817 DOIs (47.9%) return HTTP 404 from DataCite's public metadata API. As a concrete example, https://api.datacite.org/dois/10.5281/zenodo.19013315 — the DOI for the Space Ark preservation deposit (EA-ARK-01 v4.2.7) — returns HTTP 404 at the time of writing. The observed responses across the affected DOIs are consistent with a transition from Findable to Registered state, the DataCite-supported operation for retracted content. The underlying metadata remains in DataCite's systems but is no longer publicly retrievable.

- Notice: no notification was provided in advance of the action, and no opportunity to retrieve or migrate was given.

4. What I am demanding and requesting

This letter contains one demand and one request. The distinction is deliberate.

The demand is narrow and non-negotiable. It has two operative parts: the return of my own words, and the cessation of their further use within Zenodo's systems. The request, separately, is for the institutional records of the action that removed them.

#### A. Demand: the return of my authored material, and the cessation of its further use

I am asking for the return of two categories of content:

1. All files I uploaded to Zenodo.

2. All metadata I entered into Zenodo's submission fields, including every title, description, keyword, subject, related-identifier and cross-link, contributor and creator assignment, version relationship, license selection, note, and custom field I typed or set through the submission interface, across all record states (draft, published, withdrawn, versioned).

These are my own words and my own files. I am asking for what I authored.

The material is mine. Zenodo's custody of it did not transfer ownership, and Zenodo's own representations confirm that it does not own user-uploaded content. Where depositor-authored text existed only inside Zenodo's metadata system — descriptions populated with poems, related-identifier records linking deposits into a single integrated work, contributor assignments naming co-authors — the return of that text is indispensable. No independent copy exists.

In addition to the return, I require:

3. Confirmation that no portion of my account's content, metadata, moderation logs, or derived signals will be used in the training, fine-tuning, calibration, evaluation, or testing of Zenodo's content classifiers, spam classifiers, or any related automated moderation system, including in any "automatic review of similar and related user accounts" of the kind described in Zenodo's published spam-handling guidance.

4. Disclosure of whether any portion of my account's material has already been incorporated into any such training, validation, or evaluation dataset; and, if so, the erasure of that material from those datasets, with written confirmation of the erasure.

I know the value of my words. They will not be used to improve the system that erased them.

If Zenodo cannot provide the export in a standard structured format, I will accept the native or substantially equivalent record-level format in which the material is maintained. If any field or category is withheld, please identify what was withheld and the specific basis for withholding it.

I require acknowledgment of this demand within 14 calendar days, including either the requested export or a concrete production schedule, and confirmation of the cessation and disclosure required by points 3 and 4.

#### B. Request: the institutional records of the action

Separately, I request access to or summary disclosure of the records associated with the moderation and termination of my account, including:

- the criteria applied;

- the review process used;

- the basis for the termination decision;

- the operations subsequently performed on the associated DataCite metadata records, including whether the 871 affected DOIs were transitioned from Findable to Registered state or subjected to another operation, and whether the underlying metadata remains available to Zenodo or DataCite through nonpublic systems;

- and whether comparable operations have been applied to other terminated Zenodo accounts.

I recognize that these are institutional records. I ask for them in the interest of transparency. They are also my personal data, and I am exercising my rights under CERN Operational Circular No. 11 (Rev. 1) through the parallel data subject request described in Section 6 below.

5. Preservation notice

Zenodo, CERN, and the operator of the Budapest replica site are requested to preserve all recoverable records associated with the account and its deposits, including active records, replicas, logs, moderation systems, DataCite synchronization records, and material retained within backup systems.

This notice expressly includes depositor-authored text entered directly into metadata fields, and any derived signals, training-set extracts, or classifier-input snapshots produced from the account's material.

Zenodo's General Policies state that "data files are kept in multiple replicas in a distributed file system, which is backed up to tape on a nightly basis" and that "items will be retained for the lifetime of the repository." The account was terminated on June 19, 2026. Immediate preservation is therefore necessary to prevent routine retention processes from eliminating material relevant to this demand.

Please confirm that a preservation hold has been applied.

6. Basis of the demand

The primary basis for the demand and for the parallel data subject request is CERN's own internal data protection framework, which governs Zenodo as a CERN-operated service.

CERN Operational Circular No. 11 (Rev. 1), "The Processing of Personal Data at CERN," entered into force on 1 February 2026. CERN's published guidance on data subject rights describes the rights enumerated in OC 11 as follows:

"OC 11 defines [...] eight rights of data subjects. [...] These rights include accessing the personal data, obtaining copies of it, requesting changes to it, restricting the processing of it, deleting it, or receiving it in an electronic format."
"When you submit a request, CERN has 90 calendar days to provide you with a reply. [...] The controlling service has to provide a substantive response either by taking the requested action or by providing an explanation for why the request cannot be accommodated."

CERN's Office of Data Privacy further publishes that OC 11 "applies to all processing operations carried out since the introduction of the OC 11 on 1.1.2019, including data subject rights which are enforceable also for 'old' data."

The records of my Zenodo account, the metadata fields associated with the affected deposits, and the internal records of the moderation decision that produced the termination are personal data within the scope of OC 11. The contents of my account, including all bibliographic metadata I authored containing identifying information, are records of CERN's processing of my personal data through the Zenodo service.

I am exercising:

- the right of access to my personal data processed by Zenodo and CERN in connection with my account and the moderation decision;

- the right to obtain copies of that data;

- the right to receive the data in an electronic format (data portability);

- the right to object to the processing of my account material for purposes of training, improving, calibrating, or evaluating Zenodo's content classifiers, spam classifiers, or any related automated moderation system, including the "automatic review of similar and related user accounts" described in Zenodo's published spam-handling guidance;

- the right to restrict that processing during the pendency of this dispute;

- the right to erasure of any of my account material that has already been incorporated into training, validation, or evaluation datasets for the systems above;

- and, to the extent that automated decision-making contributed to the termination, the right to information about the rules applied for that decision-making.

I am submitting this request as a parallel formal data subject request through CERN's published interface. The 90-calendar-day response clock under OC 11 begins on the date the request is filed.

A secondary basis is Zenodo's General Policies v1.0, which state that no property rights are transferred to CERN upon upload, and that records and data files are preserved even after retraction from public view. These statements are at minimum admissions as to ownership and as to the technical recoverability of the material.

The demand is additionally supported by the custodial relationship Zenodo established by accepting and storing material — including text existing only within its metadata infrastructure — under the representations above, and by the ordinary expectation that records relevant to an active dispute be preserved.

7. Parallel notice and concurrent action

This letter is sent concurrently to the parties addressed above, each of whom has a defined role in matters relevant to this dispute:

- The CERN Office of Data Privacy is the centre of expertise responsible for guiding implementation of Operational Circular No. 11 and acting as the common interface enabling data subjects to exercise their rights. It is the recipient of the parallel data subject request described in Section 6.

- DataCite Support is being notified given DataCite's role in the registration and persistent identification of the affected DOIs, and given the operations performed on the public availability of the associated metadata records.

- The OpenAIRE Helpdesk is being notified given OpenAIRE's role as a co-founder of Zenodo, as a downstream harvester of Zenodo metadata, and as a body whose Content Acquisition Policy depends on the persistence and quality of source-repository metadata.

- The HUN-REN Wigner Research Centre for Physics is being notified as the operator of the Budapest replica site, where copies of CERN/Zenodo data are physically stored, for purposes of the preservation request.

If the parallel data subject request is not resolved within the 90-calendar-day period established under Operational Circular No. 11, or if the Office of Data Privacy's evaluation is unsatisfactory, the matter is subject to onward escalation to the Data Protection Commission, CERN's independent supervisory authority established by the annexes to OC 11, whose mandate includes the investigation of complaints by data subjects regardless of their connection to CERN.

I will also pursue the matter through DataCite's governance channels and through publication of the correspondence and supporting audit materials at alexanarch.org.

8. The central issue

This was not merely a refusal to host future work.

Zenodo had already accepted custody of these materials. Some existed nowhere outside Zenodo's systems. Zenodo then terminated access without first providing notice, an export, or an opportunity to migrate the archive — and, by its own published account of practice, retained the material for the purpose of training the system that classified the account.

An institution may refuse custody at the boundary. It may decide that it will no longer provide a service. But once it has accepted irreplaceable material, represented that material as persistently preserved, and become its sole custodian, termination of service cannot silently become the disappearance of the entrusted work, nor can the entrusted work silently become an input to the system that erased it. Zenodo retains technical control over the original instance of material it does not own.

I am asking for the return of the recoverable work, the cessation of its further use, the preservation of the evidence, and an accurate account of what was done.

My words are all I have. They do not belong to you. I am not leaving without them.

Lee Sharks
