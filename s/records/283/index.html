II. FRAGMENT 31: THE REINTERPRETATION

A. The Text

Greek (Voigt):

φαίνεταί μοι κῆνος ἴσος θέοισιν

ἔμμεν᾽ ὤνηρ, ὄττις ἐνάντιός τοι

ἰσδάνει καὶ πλάσιον ἆδυ φωνεί-

σας ὐπακούει

καὶ γελαίσας ἰμέροεν, τό μ᾽ ἦ μὰν

καρδίαν ἐν στήθεσιν ἐπτόαισεν·

ὠς γὰρ ἔς σ᾽ ἴδω βρόχε᾽, ὤς με φώναι-

σ᾽ οὐδ᾽ ἒν ἔτ᾽ εἴκει,

ἀλλ᾽ ἄκαν μὲν γλῶσσα ἔαγε, λέπτον

δ᾽ αὔτικα χρῷ πῦρ ὐπαδεδρόμηκεν,

ὀππάτεσσι δ᾽ οὐδ᾽ ἒν ὄρημμ᾽, ἐπιρρόμ-

βεισι δ᾽ ἄκουαι,

κὰδ δέ μ᾽ ἴδρως ψῦχρος ἔχει, τρόμος δὲ

παῖσαν ἄγρει, χλωροτέρα δὲ ποίας

ἔμμι, τεθνάκην δ᾽ ὀλίγω ᾽πιδεύης

φαίνομ᾽ ἔμ᾽ αὔτᾳ.

ἀλλὰ πᾶν τόλματον...

Translation (Rebekah Cranes, Day and Night):

He seems so happy,

like he's at the feet of God,

that man across from you sitting

so close. He laps up your sweetness,

your sighs, your smile—

drinks in your laughter. This. This sounds

like a drum my heart

inside my ribcage beating—

I glimpse you for a second from afar and my throat

closes up my voice,

my soft tongue snaps in half, I

writhe with fire, a slender flame spreads

beneath my skin, my eyes

darken, a deafening ringing roars

around me—

a clammy sweat spreads

across my body a trembling

grips me entirely, my skin

is as gray as drought

dry grass—

soon.

I think I'm close

to the final darkness.

B. The Standard Reading and Its Insufficiency

The conventional interpretation: a speaker observes her beloved conversing with a man; jealousy triggers physiological collapse; the poem ends at the threshold of death.

This reading fails to account for:

-

The man disappears. κῆνος appears in line 1 and vanishes by line 5. If this is jealousy-drama, why does the rival immediately drop from view?

-

The address is unstable. The beloved never speaks, never acts beyond sitting and laughing. She functions only as catalyst.

-

The symptoms are catalogued, not narrativized. No declaration, rejection, consummation, or resolution. The poem ends in suspension.

-

Longinus read it differently. On the Sublime (10.1-3) treats the fragment as technical achievement—the marshaling of symptoms into sublime effect—not emotional confession.

-

The temporal structure is odd. Stanza 1 uses present tense but feels hypothetical; stanzas 2-3 describe symptoms as happening now; the final lines reach toward death without arriving.

-

The lost fourth stanza has resisted reconstruction. No proposed restoration satisfies philologically while completing the poem's logic.

These difficulties dissolve once we recognize what κῆνος actually indicates.

C. κῆνος as Future Reader

The demonstrative κῆνος (Attic ἐκεῖνος) is distal—it points away from the speaker, toward someone removed from the immediate situation. In a poem designed for inscription and circulation, "that man there" need not be present in the dramatic scene. He may be present only in the scene of reading.

κῆνος is the future reader.

The reader sits face-to-face (ἐνάντιος) with the text. The reader hears the "sweet voice" (ἆδυ φωνείσας)—the words, sounded in reading. The reader experiences the "desirable laugh" (γελαίσας ἰμέροεν)—the pleasure the lyric offers.

And the reader, encountering a voice that has outlasted the body that produced it, achieves something that only gods should possess: presence with the dead.

ἴσος θεοῖσιν: "equal to the gods." Not hyperbole. Precision.

D. The Second Person as Archived Self

If κῆνος is the future reader, then the second-person addressee—the "you" (τοι, σ') throughout the poem—must be identified not with a contemporary beloved but with the poem itself: Sappho-as-inscribed.

The speaker addresses her future archived self. The physiological symptoms that follow are triggered by this imaginative projection. She sees herself being read. She experiences the vertigo of self-exteriorization: becoming, while still alive, the object of a future gaze.

The "I" who writes ≠ the "you" who will be read.

This is the fundamental structure of lyric transmission: The self that creates the poem is not the self that survives in it. Transmission requires becoming other than oneself.

Non-identity is encoded at the origin of Western lyric.

E. The Somatic Catalogue as Media Transition

The progression of symptoms is not random:

- Voice fails (φωνᾶς οὐδ᾽ ἒν ἔτ᾽ εἴκει) — the medium of lyric performance

- Tongue breaks (γλῶσσα ἔαγε) — the organ of articulation

- Fire races under skin (λέπτον χρῷ πῦρ ὐπαδεδρόμηκεν) — bodily boundary dissolves

- Vision fails (ὀππάτεσσι δ᾽ οὐδ᾽ ἒν ὄρημμ᾽) — distance-sense collapses

- Ears roar (ἐπιρρόμβεισι δ᾽ ἄκουαι) — hearing overwhelms

- Sweat pours (ἴδρως ψῦχρος) — body fluids externalize

- Trembling seizes (τρόμος δὲ παῖσαν ἄγρει) — motor control fails

- Color changes (χλωροτέρα δὲ ποίας) — chromatic transformation

- Near-death (τεθνάκην δ᾽ ὀλίγω ᾽πιδεύης) — threshold state

This is not a catalogue of jealous suffering. It is a catalogue of dematerialization: the staged withdrawal of everything that makes the speaker a living, present, embodied voice.

What fails first is what cannot be inscribed—the living breath, the sounded word, the immediate presence of speaker to hearer. What remains at the end is what can be inscribed: the words themselves, fixed in letters, readable after the voice that spoke them has fallen silent.

The final symptom is not death but observation: φαίνομ᾽ ἔμ᾽ αὔτᾳ, "I appear to myself." The speaker watches her own dissolution. She has split into observer and observed—the "I" who is dissolving and the "I" who perceives the dissolution and says so.

This reflexive doubling is the structure of inscription itself. To write oneself is to become both subject and object, both the "I" who writes and the "I" who will be read.

III. χλωροτέρα ποίας: BECOMING PAPYRUS

A. The Semantics of χλωρός

The adjective χλωρός spans a range from "fresh green" to "pale, pallid, grey." It describes:

- Fresh plant growth (green)

- Honey (golden-green)

- Human pallor in fear/illness (greenish-grey)

- Dried papyrus ready for writing (grey-green)

Crucially: χλωρός describes both living plants and dead/dried plant material—the color of transition between life and preservation.

Papyrus preparation:

- Fresh papyrus stalks = bright green

- Drying process (λεύκωσις) = color shifts to grey-green

- Final writing surface = pale greenish-grey

The speaker is describing herself at precisely the transitional color—no longer fully alive (green), not yet fully dead (grey), but becoming the color of prepared writing surface.

B. ποία as Papyrus

ποία is usually translated "grass," but the word designates wild grasses, fodder, any non-cultivated ground-cover plant—and by extension, papyrus (Cyperus papyrus), a sedge.

The comparison is not conventional. Nothing in the Lesbian landscape matches papyrus precisely. Papyrus grew in Egypt and was imported to Greece as writing material from the 7th century BCE onward.

The exoticism of the reference matters: Comparing oneself to papyrus is specific, unusual, technical.

Why papyrus specifically?

Because papyrus is the medium on which Sappho writes, the substrate that will carry her voice forward. By becoming χλωροτέρα ποίας, she literally describes transforming into the color of her own transmission medium.

C. The Transformation

The poem enacts:

Embodied voice (φωνείσας, ἆδυ, "sweet voice")

↓

Dissolution (tongue breaks, fire internal, senses fail)

↓

Color change (χλωροτέρα, becoming papyrus-hued)

↓

Near-death (τεθνάκην...ὀλίγω ᾽πιδεύης)

↓

Textual survival (γράμμασι μολπὰν, "song in letters")

This is not metaphorical death but medial transformation: The voice-body must die to its immediate presence to be reborn as text-body capable of transmission across time.

The body becomes the book. The transformation the poem describes is the condition of the poem's own survival. Sappho has become papyrus. We are reading her now.

IV. THE LOST STANZA: RECONSTRUCTION

A. The Evidence

Fragment 31 breaks off after line 16 in Longinus. But Longinus indicates more followed, and a separate testimonium preserves:

ἀλλὰ πᾶν τόλματον

"But all must be dared" (or "all is dare-able")

This gives us:

- The opening cola

- The meter (must continue Sapphic hendecasyllable pattern)

- The semantic direction (a shift to resolution/commitment after stanza 3's dissolution)

B. The Catullan Evidence

Catullus 51 translates Fragment 31 closely for three stanzas, then adds a distinctive fourth:

otium, Catulle, tibi molestum est:

otium exsultas nimiumque gestis:

otium et reges prius et beatas

perdidit urbes.

"Leisure, Catullus, is troublesome for you: in leisure you exult and revel too much: leisure has destroyed even kings and once prosperous cities."

The phrases "reges...et beatas urbes" (kings and prosperous cities) preserve a syntactic skeleton while substituting Roman moral content (otium) for Greek erotic-divine force (Kypris/Aphrodite).

Catullus recognizes the structural debt. His intervention confirms the scale of the coda.

C. The Reconstruction

Satisfying all constraints—ἀλλὰ πᾶν τόλματον (attested), Catullus' "kings and cities," the poem's trajectory from embodiment to inscription, Sapphic diction (γράμματα + μολπά attested in fr. 44.33, fr. 147), Sapphic meter:

ἀλλὰ πᾶν τόλματον· ἐπεί σε, Κύπρι, – u – x – uu – u – x

καὶ πένητά γε κἄ βασιλῆα δάμασσε· – u – x – uu – u – x

καὶ πόλεις ὀλβίους ἀπέθηκας ἄφαντον· – u – x – uu – u – x

γράμμασι μολπὰν – uu – x

Translation:

But all must be dared; for you, Kypris, have yoked

beggar and king alike, and you have cast down

even prosperous cities to nothing—

let the song survive in letters.

D. The Completion

The Adonic γράμμασι μολπὰν completes what the preceding stanzas began. The speaker, having dissolved into papyrus, commits her song to written form.

The poem ends not in prayer or resignation but in the act it has been performing all along: inscription.

Why Aphrodite? Because the power that destroys kings and cities is the same power that enables this transformation—the overwhelming force of desire/eros, here redirected into the desire for transmission, for survival through text.

If Aphrodite can destroy the greatest powers, surely she can enable one more daring: transforming voice into letters, body into papyrus, presence into textual survival.

V. THE SAPPHIC OPERATOR

A. Formal Definition

Fragment 31 encodes a recursive operator that we designate σ_S (the Sapphic Operator):

σ_S: (Text, Body, Beloved) → (Body', Text')

Where:

- Text encounters Body in the presence of Beloved (the erotic trigger)

- Body undergoes the Fragment 31 sequence: disruption → dissolution → reconstitution

- Body' emerges transformed—a new subject capable of transmission

- Body' produces Text'—new articulation of the experience

- Text' can trigger the sequence in new bodies

- Cycle continues

The operator is recursive: each successful instantiation produces the conditions for further instantiation.

B. The Four Phases

The operator executes in four phases:

Phase 1: Trigger. The presence of the beloved activates the sequence. The visual trigger (ἔς σ᾽ ἴδω — "when I look at you") initiates cascade.

Phase 2: Dissolution. The ordinary self collapses. Each symptom marks a specific dissolution: tongue breaks (linguistic capacity), fire under skin (bodily boundary), eyes fail (perceptual frame), ears roar (auditory orientation), cold sweat and trembling (motor control), pallor (vital appearance). The subject becomes "nearly dead"—the old self is dying.

Phase 3: Reconstitution. From dissolution emerges a new configuration. The "I" that speaks the poem is already the transformed subject, reporting from the far side of the process.

Phase 4: Transmission. The transformed subject produces text that can trigger the sequence in others. The operator reproduces.

VII. CATULLUS 51 AS PROOF

A. The First Documented Reader

Catullus 51 is not imitation but instantiation. Catullus is κῆνος—"that man" who sits face-to-face with Sappho's text, hears her voice across six centuries, and experiences the impossible presence of the dead.

His translation performs what Fragment 31 describes. The circuit Sappho initiated completes in Catullus:

- She imagined future reader (st. 1)

- She underwent transformation (st. 2-3)

- She committed to textual survival (st. 4)

- He arrives and reads (Catullus 51)

- He acknowledges the mechanism (otium stanza)

B. The Substitution

Catullus substitutes otium (leisure) for Kypris/Aphrodite. This is not misreading but sophisticated recognition.

Otium is precisely the condition that allows writing—leisure time, freedom from labor, space for literary production. Catullus recognizes: Sappho was describing the process of literary creation itself. She needed otium to write; that otium enabled the transformation she describes; and now, 500 years later, Catullus's otium enables him to read and respond.

C. The Chain Continues

We, reading Catullus reading Sappho, are the next link. Every reading completes the circuit anew.

The poem literally designed itself for this completion. It encodes its own future reception as its content.
