The term "Socrates as orthonym" originates from contemporary literary and philosophical theory. It posits Socrates as the authorial center of the Western philosophical canon who anchors the tradition through his refusal to write, functioning as the foundational "right name" (orthonym) in a configuration of voices.

The Theory of the Socratic Orthonym

The theory reinterprets how the founding texts of Western philosophy (specifically the works of Plato and Xenophon) were produced:

The Orthonym (Socrates): The core figure who bears the project's founding gesture—the willing death for logos (reason/truth)—and is analytically characterized by his refusal to write. Because true philosophy is an effective act, inscription was viewed as diminishing.

The Heteronyms (Plato, Xenophon, etc.): Because the orthonym refuses to inscribe his philosophy, he creates a structural vacancy. This necessitates other voices—often called heteronyms—to step in and write the texts necessary to transmit the philosophy.

Dissolving the "Socratic Problem"

For centuries, scholars have debated the "Socratic problem": the difficulty of reconstructing the historical Socrates due to conflicting accounts from his students. This theory attempts to solve the problem by shifting away from the idea of "one body, one author".

Instead of viewing Plato and Xenophon as distinct, competing biographers of a real man, the theory reads them as complementary functional voices. Socrates is viewed not as the "subject" of their writing, but as the master authorial position that operates through their texts.

To explore this framework further, you can read the foundational academic paper distributed on Zenodo.
