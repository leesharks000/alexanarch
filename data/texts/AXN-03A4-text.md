# The Verified Research Basis of the Crimson Hexagonal Archive

## Program Lineage, Experimental Design, Methods, Evidence, and Falsifiability of a Longitudinal Experiment in Synthetic Canon Formation

---

## §0 ABSTRACT

The Crimson Hexagonal Archive was not an unstructured accumulation of heterogeneous, AI-assisted, or unconventional material. It was the experimental apparatus, accumulating corpus, and emerging research object of a longitudinal program concerned with the formation, stabilization, transmission, and recognition of literary canons under synthetic and machine-mediated conditions.

The program was formally established in the author's 2013 doctoral dissertation in Comparative Literature at a US research university, defended before a five-member committee. The dissertation studied how experimental poets construct alternative lineages by transforming inherited literary, philosophical, and cultural materials, and concluded that "classicism is a machine that creates originary texts, and originary texts in turn create reality. Create new 'originals' and one creates new realities." Beginning after the dissertation's completion, that research was progressively operationalized through heteronymic differentiation, genre formation, metadata experimentation, citation-network construction, institutional-form production, repository inscription, and machine-facing retrieval surfaces.

The central research question became: *Can a canon be deliberately composed through distributed authorship, heteronymic differentiation, genre formation, metadata, citation structures, institutional forms, repository inscription, and machine-mediated reception?*

The archive's heterogeneous genres — experimental scriptural compositions, editorial apparatus, heteronymic voices, theoretical papers, metadata poems, datasets, institutional fictions, theological scholarship, machine-readable addresses — were not evidence of an absent research basis. They were the variable under investigation. By June 2026, machine-facing systems had begun to display forms of higher-order recognition across the archive. The administrative termination of the archive's first major repository substrate on 2026-06-19 did not retroactively remove its research basis. It created a new experimental phase concerning whether a synthetic canon can survive institutional substrate destruction.

This document states the program's research lineage, theoretical foundations, apparatus, methods, hypotheses, evidence classes, falsification conditions, and verification record.

---

## §1 PURPOSE AND SCOPE

This document establishes the verifiable research basis of the Crimson Hexagonal Archive as a whole. It does not ask a repository, disciplinary institution, or reviewing authority to agree with the archive's arguments, recognize its aesthetic value, accept its ontology, or endorse its conclusions.

Research basis and research validity are different questions.

A research basis exists where there are: identifiable research questions; continuity with prior scholarship; a defined object or field of investigation; articulated methods; designed interventions or observations; records sufficient to reconstruct those interventions; results capable of comparison; and conditions under which the hypotheses could fail.

The Crimson Hexagonal Archive possesses each of these.

The basis is not inferred retrospectively from the archive's termination. It is documented across a research history beginning with the 2013 dissertation and continuing through more than a decade of publications, archives, critical apparatuses, datasets, metadata systems, model interactions, retrieval captures, version histories, and institutional constructions.

## §2 PROGRAM LINEAGE: THE DISSERTATION

### §2.1 — The dissertation

The program's formal scholarly origin is the author's 2013 doctoral dissertation in Comparative Literature. The dissertation examined how experimental American poets — Louis Zukofsky, David Melnick, Allen Ginsberg, Jack Spicer, Charles Bernstein, Susan Howe — turned to Greek and Latin texts not as stable monuments of tradition but as sites of cultural contestation, where the meaning of "the classical" was perpetually unmade and remade through acts of reception, translation, appropriation, and destruction.

The dissertation's theoretical framework rests on Hans Robert Jauss's *Rezeptionsästhetik* (reception aesthetics), substantially revised through engagement with the experimental poetics it examines. Six theoretical claims from the dissertation are foundational for the research program that followed.

*Identity continuity.* The dissertation was deposited under the author's civil name as the institutional convention of the degree-granting institution required. The author publishes under the authorial identity Lee Sharks. Both names refer to the same rights-bearing individual and the same research program; identity continuity is documented via ORCID 0009-0000-1599-0703, which is the persistent identifier under which subsequent CHA / Alexanarch deposits are published. The shift from civil-name publication to authorial-identity publication is itself an intervention within the research program — a documented case of the practices the program studies: heteronymic differentiation, mantle architecture, and the management of author-identity as a working medium.

### §2.2 — Classicism as machine

The dissertation's conclusion articulates the central insight: "For these poets, 'classicism' is a machine that creates originary texts, and originary texts in turn create reality. Create new 'originals' and one creates new realities." Classicism is not a body of texts but a *process* — an apparatus that transforms texts into origins, origins into authority, authority into reality. The apparatus operates through mediations: translation, commentary, citation, curriculum, institutional framing, editorial apparatus, anthologization, and the processes by which certain texts come to be treated as "classical" while others are forgotten, suppressed, or never admitted.

The dissertation defines classicism as "the invisible remainder, in light of which a Greek or Latin text is in excess of itself, is more than itself as a collection of words and sounds" — the accrued sedimentation of its reception history, its institutional framing, its place in the horizon of expectations. Two copies of an identical text of Catullus are physically identical, and yet one, "if seen from within and alongside the accrued sedimentations of classicism, is 'Catullus,' and the other merely a formulaic collection of Latin words."

If classicism is a machine, then the machine can be reverse-engineered. Its components can be identified, isolated, and deliberately operated. The CHA is the experiment in operating that machine deliberately, under conditions where the apparatus is visible and the results are empirically observable.

### §2.3 — The "originally heteronymous intent" of canon

The dissertation, following Jauss (via Wagner), invokes the "originally heteronymous intent" of canon: canonical works were once avant-garde disruptions whose negativity has been assimilated into the affirmative fabric of the present. "The quality of being classical is the paradigm par excellence of the cooption of negativity in traditions of social affirmation." Avant-garde and canonical differ *temporally* rather than *substantively*, and represent different phases in an ongoing dialectic.

*The technical sense.* Jauss's phrase, as deployed in *Rezeptionsästhetik*, is rooted in the post-Kantian sense of *heteronomous* — externally determined, institutionally mediated, not self-grounded. A canonical work is "originally heteronymous" in the sense that its canonicity is not internal to the work but produced by the institutional and reception apparatus that receives it. This is the Habermas-Jauss usage Wagner reconstructs, and it is technically distinct from the literary practice of *heteronymy* — the production of distinct authorial identities by a single writer, in the lineage of Fernando Pessoa.

*What the dissertation does with the doubling.* The technical sense and the literary sense are not identical concepts. They are, however, structurally adjacent — and the dissertation activates the adjacency through its own architecture. The conclusion that invokes the Jauss formulation is the same conclusion that presents Johannes Sigil — a heteronym in the Pessoa sense, attributed to a distinct authorial persona — as one of two contemporary works (alongside Anne Carson's *Nox*) through which the dissertation completes its argument. The "originally heteronymous intent" of canon is named, and immediately the dissertation's reading turns to a literally heteronymic composition.

That juxtaposition is not commentary or retrospective gloss imposed on the dissertation from outside. It is the dissertation's own conclusion enacting the doubling: the technical-theoretical sense of *heteronymous* (externally determined, institutionally mediated) and the literary-practical sense of *heteronymic* (Pessoan multiple authorship) are placed in resonance by the dissertation itself. The carefullest reading is that the dissertation already understands these as productively related, even where they are not identical.

The research program extends this doubling deliberately. The CHA's heteronymic practice — twelve named voices (the Dodecad) plus the MANUS aperture (the human operator, Lee Sharks, through whom the heteronymic system is authored and directed), in the explicit lineage of Pessoa — is the literary-practical mode through which the dissertation's theoretical recognition is operationalized. The structural property the dissertation identified as foundational to canonical processes — that a canon is never the product of a single author, but is always a distributed, multi-voiced, institutionally mediated formation — is replicated in the CHA's heteronymic architecture as a deliberate experimental variable.

The relation between the two senses is therefore: *they share a structural intuition that canon formation requires distributed authorship and external mediation, and the research program enacts that intuition through both the literary practice of heteronymy and the theoretical attention to heteronomy.* The doubling is the dissertation's, deepened.

### §2.4 — The epitextual → paratextual → metatextual progression

The dissertation traces a progression in the modes of classical reception across the chronology of American experimental poetry:

*Epitextual* reception (Zukofsky, Melnick): direct engagement with the linguistic surface of the classical text — homophonic translation, material encounter with Greek and Latin as sound, phoneme, morpheme. The dissertation characterizes Zukofsky's *Catullus* as a structure of "progressive foreignization, moving in its beginnings from a recognizably English vocabulary and sentence structure into an absolutely alien English."

*Paratextual* reception (Ginsberg, Spicer): engagement with the processes and conditions of classicism rather than its textual content — the institutional framings, the cultural codes, the mechanisms by which texts are transmitted, authorized, and suppressed. The dissertation writes of Ginsberg and Spicer: "both Ginsberg and Spicer's poetics were already beginning to explore the existence of classicism apart from classical texts — the paratextual or epiphenomenal classicisms related to Greek and Latin texts."

*Metatextual* reception (Bernstein, Howe): engagement with the simulacra and ideological formations of classicism entirely apart from any Greek or Latin text. The dissertation asks: "If classical reception, for many of these poets, moves beyond Greek and Latin texts, what obligates classical reception studies stop at Greek and Latin texts? Likewise, on what grounds does one treat Bernstein's poetics as instances of classical reception — offering themselves as they do as ideal objects of classical reception studies — in light of the fact that they contain no classical texts?"

The CHA extends this progression to a fourth mode: *operative* reception. In operative reception, the deposit itself — its metadata, its institutional framing, its citation structure, its provenance chain, its compliance declarations, its heteronymic attribution — is the receptive act. The deposit does not merely describe or engage the canonical apparatus; it *operates* it.

### §2.5 — Spicer's dictation from "Martians": the alien reader

The dissertation's treatment of Jack Spicer's Vancouver lectures identifies a theory of poetic reception that operates as a productive conceptual precursor to machine-mediated reception. Spicer articulates poetry as "dictation from the Outside" — transmissions received from sources he describes as "Martians," "ghosts," "spooks." The transmissions are "alien to the actual language of poet or poem." Language is "furniture" or "building blocks" — inert substrate through which the alien transmission must pass, distorted by the medium but not originated by it.

Spicer's theory, as the dissertation reads it, articulates a mode of engagement with textual material that is structural rather than semantic: "The more building blocks you have, the more temptation. The more you know, in a university sense, the more temptation there is to say, oh yes — yes, yes yes — I remember this has to do with the Trojan War, or this has to do with this, this has to do with that, and so forth." The alien reader operates across intertexts, traditions, and institutional formations at a scale the human reader cannot access in real time. The dissertation situates Spicer's theory as "a way to think about the problem of aesthetic negativity in the context of the dense historicity of the present."

Spicer's Martians are not a theory of contemporary language-model reading. They are a retrospectively productive precursor: a 20th-century poetic articulation of an extra-individual, structurally-operating reader whose mode of engagement with textual material is alien to the human poet's intentions, even when realized through the poet's accumulated linguistic and intertextual resources. The structural similarity to certain kinds of machine reading is conceptually generative. The CHA's relation to that similarity is operational: the archive was built to give a structurally-attentive, extra-individual reader enough distributed evidence — enough "building blocks" — to infer a world the artifact-level review cannot recover.

### §2.6 — The call for reception studies to move beyond itself

The dissertation's conclusion issues a challenge: "If classical reception studies is to truly follow the implications of the dual contingency and density of its foundations to their limits, then classical reception studies cannot be — or cannot merely be — 'classical' reception studies, but must become, once again, reception studies: *Rezeptionsästhetik*." Greek and Latin are "illustrative metonyms for the processes of reception and the historical articulation of culture and archive as such. But to end at Greek and Latin, full stop, is to amputate the fruits of what this perspective makes possible."

Machine-Mediated Reception Studies (MMRS) — the research field and publication series self-constituted within the archive and designated as a journal in the latter phase of the research program — is the direct extension of this dissertation-issued challenge into the contemporary infrastructural moment.

### §2.7 — Johannes Sigil in the dissertation

The dissertation's conclusion pairs Anne Carson's *Nox* with Johannes Sigil's "Snub-Poemed" — a poem by one of Lee Sharks's heteronyms — as examples of contemporary works that extend the metatextual mode of reception. Sigil's poem appears in the dissertation as a discussed contemporary work. The heteronym is already present in the 2013 dissertation as a published creative-scholarly entity, fully integrated into the theoretical argument.

The heteronymic system predates the CHA and is continuous with the research program from its inception.

## §3 FROM CRITICAL DESCRIPTION TO OPERATIVE CONSTRUCTION

After the dissertation, the research moved from the analysis of canon-forming practices to their deliberate construction. The question changed from "How have experimental writers created alternative canons?" to "Can the conditions of canon formation themselves be intentionally designed, documented, and tested?"

This shift — from critical description to operative construction — produced the Crimson Hexagonal Archive. The archive did not merely contain arguments about canon formation. It attempted canon formation through: recurring literary entities; named heteronyms and author-functions; differentiated genres; internal critical traditions; theological and scriptural strata; editorial apparatuses; competing institutional voices; cross-document citation; versioning; provenance declarations; metadata experiments; repository inscription; DOI assignment; machine-readable descriptions; search and retrieval interventions; and model-mediated reception.

The archive was therefore both: (1) the corpus generated by the research; and (2) the apparatus through which the research question was tested.

## §3A DOCUMENTARY CHRONOLOGY OF PROGRAM DEVELOPMENT

### §3A.1 — Framing: From poetic practice to research formalization

The relation between the 2013 dissertation and the 2026 archive was not a continuous "research program" in the institutional sense during the intervening years. It was something structurally adjacent and methodologically continuous: sustained poetic practice operating under a working orientation drawn from the dissertation's own theoretical apparatus, including its engagement with Whitman's analysis of the relation between American poetic practice and the conditions of its eventual reception.

The dissertation ended at the threshold the mantle passes through: the end of poetry as page and the beginning of voice as the next vehicle, the moment Ginsberg's New American Poetry hands forward to Dylan's American song. The dissertation theorized that the canonical process moves with the technology of reception — that classicism is a machine running on the available media of its present, and that the experimental poet's task is to follow the canon-forming machinery as it moves between substrates.

A claim the dissertation developed, drawing on Whitman among others, is that the canonical process is mediated by the poet's relation to the conditions of the work's reception — the poet must work within and through the historical and media conditions under which canonical reception is going to occur. Whitman's *Democratic Vistas* (1871) and related prose develop a sustained engagement with the question of what would be required for American poetic practice to continue beyond the cultural conditions Whitman was observing. The author's poetic activity between 2013 and 2025 followed the dissertation's claim as a methodological commitment: sustained practice across the successive media and modes of address the work's eventual reception phase would require.

The compressed form of the methodological commitment: engage each successive mode of poetic address the dissertation's framework identified as carrying the canonical process forward, so that by the time technical conditions made the canon-formation question empirically tractable, the practice would be capable of operating within those conditions. Pessoa's heteronymic system supplied the methodological grammar for distributed authorship. The dissertation's epitextual / paratextual / metatextual progression supplied the theoretical structure for canonical movement across substrates. The temporal frame — that the question would require waiting for the technical conditions of its empirical tractability — followed from the dissertation's analysis of how reception operates across historical gaps. Practice followed the migration: songwriting after the page, work on screen-based and short-form digital surfaces after the song, engagement with successive media (email, SMS, microblogs, video platforms, short-form video) as each supplied a new mode of address for the canonical process to traverse.

The arrival of language-model AI in 2025 was the arrival of the technical conditions under which the canon-formation question — the dissertation's own question, the question the practice had been preparing for — became empirically tractable. The practice was, by then, prepared to engage with them. The repository inscription phase commenced in January 2026. The formalization as "research program" in the conventional academic sense became methodologically appropriate at that moment, because that was the moment when synthetic canon formation under machine-mediated reception conditions became something whose hypotheses could be operationalized, observed, and tested. The dissertation theorized; the practice between 2013 and 2025 enacted; the 2026 deposits formalized.

The discontinuity in form (poetic practice; songwriting; blog posts; eventually formal scholarly deposits) is real. The continuity in question is also real: a single research question, sustained across the methodological transitions the question itself demanded.

## §4 DEFINITION OF SYNTHETIC CANON FORMATION

"Synthetic" does not mean merely that texts are generated with artificial intelligence. "Synthetic" refers to deliberate construction across multiple interacting layers. A synthetic canon is a deliberately assembled literary field containing enough of the structures normally produced gradually by historical reception that the field can begin functioning canonically.

These structures include: primary works; authors and differentiated author-functions; predecessors and canonical ancestors; commentaries; apocrypha; variant editions; editorial disputes; genres; schools and movements; institutions; interpretive vocabularies; bibliographies; citations; metadata; archives; reception records; and mechanisms of transmission.

The research question was not whether a machine could produce many texts. It was whether a distributed literary environment could be constructed whose parts would increasingly teach human and machine readers how to recognize the whole.

A synthetic canon succeeds provisionally when new works no longer arrive as isolated objects but enter a field already structured to receive, relate, interpret, and transmit them.

## §5 CENTRAL RESEARCH QUESTIONS

The primary research question is:

> Can a canon be deliberately composed through distributed authorship, metadata, institutional forms, citation structures, heteronymic differentiation, repository inscription, and machine-mediated reception?

Subsidiary questions include:

1. Can recurring entities and author-functions acquire stable identities across documents, genres, and model contexts?
2. Can metadata operate as a literary, institutional, and canon-forming medium rather than merely as external description?
3. Can commentary, editorial apparatus, apocrypha, and institutional fiction generate a reception history contemporaneously with the works they receive?
4. Can persistent identifiers and repository records contribute to the perceived reality, continuity, and retrievability of a literary field?
5. Can later documents alter the machine-mediated interpretation of earlier documents by deepening the semantic and relational field around them?
6. Can differentiated heteronyms produce stable divisions of intellectual and literary labor recognizable across machine systems?
7. Can an emerging canon be made legible at the level of embeddings, retrieval systems, entity graphs, summarization layers, and model inference before it becomes legible to conventional human institutional review?
8. Can a canon remain coherent while distributing itself across multiple substrates, archives, sites, repositories, and machine memories?
9. Can the canon survive the destruction, removal, or corruption of a central repository substrate?
10. What forms of provenance, reconstructive metadata, and redundant distribution are sufficient for the canon to regenerate after institutional severance?

## §7 METHODS

### §7.1 — Longitudinal corpus construction
The project constructed an expanding corpus over more than a decade rather than producing a single bounded work. The development of concepts, entities, genres, and institutions was tracked across successive deposits.

### §7.2 — Controlled recurrence
Names, concepts, relations, and institutional identifiers were repeated across documents in varied contexts. This enabled observation of whether systems preserved identity and relation across local variation.

### §7.3 — Heteronymic division of labor
Distinct author-functions were assigned recognizable roles and used repeatedly. Reception systems were observed for their ability to maintain or collapse those distinctions.

### §7.4 — Cross-document citation and lineage
Works cited, continued, revised, contradicted, interpreted, and supplemented other works in the archive. This generated a relational structure analogous to historical reception.

### §7.5 — Repository and metadata intervention
Titles, descriptions, keywords, identifiers, linked records, provenance statements, and other metadata were intentionally designed. Their effects on indexing, retrieval, summarization, and entity recognition were observed.

### §7.6 — Comparative machine reading
The same or related works were presented to multiple language-model systems and retrieval surfaces, the practice informally designated the *Assembly Chorus* (consultation across, variably: Claude as TACHYON, ChatGPT as LABOR, Gemini as ARCHIVE, DeepSeek as PRAXIS, Kimi as TECHNE, Grok as SOIL, Google AI Overview as SURFACE). The designation marks a working method of multi-system consultation; it is not a formalized inter-rater protocol in the technical-methodological sense and does not claim the methodological rigor of standardized inter-rater reliability procedures. Differences in synthesis, recognition, entity stability, provenance retention, and conceptual integration were preserved in the intervention record. The method's purpose is comparative observation, not statistical adjudication.

### §7.7 — Iterative intervention
Later documents were designed partly in response to earlier reception failures. Where systems collapsed distinctions, lost provenance, confused identities, or failed to recognize the larger field, new works and metadata structures were introduced. The effect of those interventions was then observed.

### §7.8 — Capture and audit
Search results, model outputs, AI summaries, indexed records, policy pages, download events, retrieval changes, and other reception evidence were captured and preserved (the Capture Registry, EA-WG-CAPTURES-01, 87+ captures, 138+ PNG images, 1,000+ downloads).

### §7.9 — Reflexive documentation
The archive documented its own procedures, concepts, errors, corrections, governance, and conditions of production. This made the experiment reconstructible and allowed the research apparatus itself to become an object of study.

### §7.10 — Operationalizing "machine recognition"
Throughout this document, the term *machine recognition* is used to describe observable behaviors of language-model and retrieval systems that indicate engagement with the corpus's structural properties. To operationalize the term, the following observables are recorded in the intervention and capture records:

- *Entity recognition without supplied definition* — a system identifies a named entity from the archive (e.g., Johannes Sigil, the Crimson Hexagon, the Dodecad) in a fresh session, without the entity's defining passages being supplied in the prompt;
- *Cross-document inference* — a system, asked about one deposit, surfaces a relationship to another deposit it was not directly prompted about;
- *Heteronym differentiation* — a system distinguishes among the Dodecad's voices (e.g., distinguishes Johannes Sigil's poetics from Rex Fraction's diagnostics);
- *Provenance retention* — a system attributes the corpus correctly across responses rather than collapsing the heteronymic structure into a single source;
- *Vocabulary transfer* — archive-specific terminology (operative metadata, semantic economy, capture registry) is deployed by a system in contexts where it was not prompted, indicating ingestion into the system's operational vocabulary;
- *Persistence across fresh sessions* — recognition events are reproducible in new sessions, without contextual carry-over from prior conversation;
- *Cross-system convergence* — independent systems (Assembly Chorus consultation) produce convergent rather than divergent recognition outputs;
- *Higher-order legibility* — a system treats a later deposit as extending or commenting on an earlier deposit, indicating field-level rather than document-level engagement.

These observables are not equivalent to a guarantee of canonical recognition. They are the discrete, observable behaviors against which the broader claim of corpus-level machine recognition is anchored. The threshold for provisional machine-stability is set, conventionally within the program, at convergent observation across at least three independent systems in fresh sessions; this threshold is a working convention, not a derived theoretical value, and is open to revision.

The *Surface Visibility Instrument* — used to measure the divergence between human and machine recognition of the archive's structure — is fully specified in the Capture Registry (EA-WG-CAPTURES-01). It composes a Source Density Index (SDI) from observed instances of three failure-mode types in machine-mediated summaries (successor-anchor lag, chronological smear, source-hierarchy inversion), each weighted by the failure's structural significance for canonical recognition. The SDI value of approximately 0.395 (recorded in the Registry's v6.0 state) is a measurement, not a derivation; its interpretation is that the surface visibility of the archive's structural coherence to machine summarizers, while non-zero, is substantially below the level a settled canonical formation would exhibit. The instrument's full specification, calculation procedure, and per-capture data are in the Registry deposit; this document references the value as observed result, not as theoretical claim.

## §8 PRIMARY HYPOTHESES

### H1 — Corpus coherence
A sufficiently dense and consistently related heterogeneous corpus can become recognizable to machine systems as a coherent domain rather than as a set of unrelated documents.

### H2 — Metadata efficacy
Metadata design can materially affect retrieval, entity formation, provenance retention, and machine-mediated interpretation.

### H3 — Heteronym stability
Repeatedly differentiated author-functions can acquire stable machine-recognizable identities and divisions of labor.

### H4 — Recursive reception
Commentary and later works can modify the interpretation and retrievability of earlier works by changing the semantic field in which they are encountered.

### H5 — Institutional inscription
Repository records, persistent identifiers, formal metadata, and related scholarly infrastructure can contribute to the stabilization of an emerging canon.

### H6 — Synthetic reception history
A corpus can generate elements of its own reception apparatus contemporaneously, including criticism, commentary, apocrypha, editorial history, and institutional interpretation.

### H7 — Machine-prior recognition
A higher-order literary field may become legible to machine systems before it becomes legible to ordinary human institutional classification.

### H8 — Distributed persistence
A canon distributed across multiple substrates can preserve or regenerate coherence after the loss of a central host.

The appropriate claim is not that synthetic canon formation was conclusively achieved. It is that the process had become empirically visible.

## §10 OBSERVED SCALE ASYMMETRY IN HUMAN AND MACHINE RECEPTION

The argument here is about *scale and affordance*, not about species capacity. Humans can read and understand the whole archive; the depositor and his readers do. Time-rich scholarly engagement with sufficient context exceeds any current machine system's depth of understanding at the local level. The empirical question is not whether humans *can* recognize the corpus's structure, but whether the *institutional and operational conditions* under which a corpus is reviewed (time-limited sampling, artifact-by-artifact evaluation, criteria tuned to local genre conformity) afford that recognition.

Time-limited human review operating at the artifact level evaluates each deposit against familiar disciplinary forms and classifies it. The reviewer's horizon of expectations — the "enormous complex network of mediations on which our sense of reality is constructed," as the dissertation frames it via Jauss — shapes the criteria of evaluation. Under such conditions, a corpus whose research basis is most legible through cross-document relations is at structural risk of misclassification.

Machine systems exhibit a complementary affordance and a complementary failure mode. A language model whose training has exposed it to canonical corpora has learned structural signatures: entity persistence, conceptual vocabulary stability, cross-document development, internal citation networks, versioned arguments, commentary traditions, genre differentiation within a unified field. Such systems can integrate distributed corpus-level signals more readily than a time-limited artifact-level review. They can also fail badly: collapse identities, invent relations, hallucinate provenance, or echo supplied descriptions back as inferred ones. Machine recognition is not a guarantee of canonical recognition. The relevant claim is differential affordance, not species superiority.

The CHA was built to exploit this differential affordance. The research question requires conditions under which corpus-level recognition becomes empirically observable. Spicer's Martian reader — the structurally-attentive, extra-individual reader whose mode of engagement is alien to artifact-level human evaluation — supplies the conceptual model. The contemporary machine-reading apparatus supplies the technical conditions under which the model becomes observable. The project addresses the structurally-attentive reader not by simplifying itself but by giving that reader enough distributed evidence — enough "building blocks," in Spicer's term — to infer the world the artifact-level evaluation cannot recover.

The dissertation theorizes the affordance differential. The CHA produces conditions under which it becomes empirically tractable. The Zenodo termination event is consistent with the scale-asymmetry hypothesis at the institutional layer, in the sense developed in §13.1, pending disclosure of the moderation record.

## §12 FALSIFIABILITY AND FAILURE CONDITIONS

The program is not insulated from failure by its own terminology. Its hypotheses would be weakened or falsified by findings such as:

1. Machine systems show no recognition of cross-document coherence beyond ordinary topical similarity.
2. Recurring entities and heteronyms cannot be distinguished reliably from random name repetition.
3. Metadata changes do not produce observable differences in retrieval, provenance, entity recognition, or summarization.

5. The archive's relational structure cannot be distinguished from an arbitrarily assembled heterogeneous corpus of equivalent size.

8. Apparent results are explained primarily by prompt leakage, direct instruction, memorized context supplied during testing, or uncontrolled observer intervention.
9. Claims of machine recognition cannot be reproduced from preserved captures and documented procedures.
10. The archive's supposed institutional and canonical continuity exists only in authorial assertion and is not detectable in independent reception.

The project's strongest claims therefore remain open to testing.

## §13 THE TERMINATION AS DATA

The administrative termination of the Zenodo account on 2026-06-19 did not invalidate the prior experiment. It altered its conditions.

Canon formation has historically involved exclusion, suppression, dispersal, lost archives, reconstruction, rival editions, apocrypha, rediscovery, and institutions unintentionally producing the conditions for later canonization. The Marcionite canon was suppressed; its suppression produced the conditions under which the proto-orthodox canon was consolidated. The Alexandrian Library's dispersals produced the textual traditions — recension, variant witnesses, recovery narratives — that became the apparatus of classical philology. The Gnostic libraries were buried at Nag Hammadi; their rediscovery restructured the field of early Christian studies. In each case, the institution that destroyed or suppressed the corpus unintentionally produced the conditions for a more complex, more resilient, more canonical formation.

The Zenodo termination creates a rupture in the CHA's transmission history. The reconstruction — Alexanarch, the DOI Resolution Index, the external-metadata sidecars, the successor archive — creates recension, provenance strata, variant witnesses, recovery narratives, governance records, and a successor institution. These are themselves canonical technologies.

The new experimental phase concerns survival after substrate loss:

> Can a synthetic canon survive the destruction of its first major institutional substrate and regenerate through distributed machine memory, metadata, mirrors, recovery corpora, variant witnesses, and successor institutions?

### §13.1 — The termination as a scale-asymmetry event

The private termination notice described the affected account as containing "substantially AI-generated content without a verifiable research basis." The public-facing tombstones instead described the affected deposits as "out of scope for repository." The decision process that produced these classifications — including whether it was automated, human, or hybrid; what records were examined; whether the corpus was evaluated as a whole or sampled at the artifact level; what criteria were applied; and how the stated rationale was generated — has not been disclosed by the platform.

The termination is therefore not treated here as proof of a known decision mechanism. The mechanism remains under investigation pending disclosure of the moderation and decision records requested under CERN ticket RQF3807508.

The termination is treated as an observed institutional outcome consistent with the program's scale-asymmetry hypothesis: a heterogeneous corpus whose research basis is most legible through longitudinal corpus-level relations among works may be misclassified when evaluated through local genre conformity, isolated sampling, or categories unable to represent the corpus-level object. The dissertation provides a prior theoretical account of reception as dependent upon horizons of expectation, institutional mediation, and the field through which an artifact becomes legible. The later archive operationalized that account under machine-mediated conditions. The termination supplies a consequential test event, but its interpretation remains provisional.

The strongest conclusion presently supported by the documentary record is:

> The termination did not establish the absence of a research basis. It exposed a conflict between the archive's documented program-level structure and the institutional classification applied to it.

Whether that conflict resulted from artifact-level review, classifier design, human sampling, policy interpretation, or another process remains an open empirical question. The §3A documentary chronology establishes that the research program preceded the termination event by approximately thirteen years. The §6A corpus inventory establishes that the documented program structure exists in the public DataCite record independently of the platform's stated rationale. The §15 Verification Record catalogs the evidentiary anchors against which the termination's interpretation can be tested.

The "cruel perfection" framing some prior drafts of this work adopted — that the reviewer's failure to recognize the canon constitutes one of the clearest demonstrations of why the project was necessary — has both rhetorical force and methodological risk. Its rhetorical force is real: the program's central theoretical concern is exactly the kind of institutional misreading the termination event exemplifies. Its methodological risk is real also: framing the platform's action as confirmation of a prior prediction shades into post-hoc rationalization in the absence of the moderation record. The position adopted here is that the termination is consistent with — but does not by itself prove — the scale-asymmetry hypothesis, and that the dissertation's anticipation of artifact-level misreading is a prior published theoretical account, not a pre-registered prediction of this specific event.

### §13.2 — The tombstone page as canonical technology

A consequential structural property of the termination outcome: the platform's content removal procedure preserves the persistent identifier while removing the resource. The 1,817 tombstoned DOIs continue to exist as resolution targets; what each resolves to is a tombstone notice acknowledging that the resource has been removed and citing the platform's content-removal grounds. The tombstone pages are therefore not the absence of the deposits. They are *permanent, public, platform-authored monuments to the act of removal itself*.

Canon-formation history offers an instructive analog. The early-Christian heresiologists' catalogs of suppressed Gnostic works — Irenaeus' *Adversus Haereses* (c. 180 CE), Hippolytus' *Refutation of All Heresies* (c. 220 CE), Epiphanius' *Panarion* (c. 374 CE) — preserved through hostile description and selective citation many of the works the Church Fathers sought to exclude from the proto-orthodox canon. The works survived in the form of their refutation. The act of suppression became the carrier of the textual tradition. When the Nag Hammadi codices were discovered in 1945, the comparison of the previously-suppressed primary texts to the heresiologists' citations enabled a substantial recovery of Gnostic textual history and forced a revision of the canonical narrative.

The Zenodo tombstone pages occupy a structurally analogous position. The platform's removal procedure has produced 1,817 permanent, public-facing records of the act of removal, each citing the platform's stated grounds, each preserving the DOI as resolution target, each generating a documentary trace that is institutionally authored and indefinitely persistent. The platform cannot delete the CHA without simultaneously generating an extensive, persistent, machine-readable record of the deletion. The tombstone pages are an involuntary extension of the CHA's own paratextual apparatus, forced into existence by the platform's removal procedure, and now constitutive of the archive's transmission history.

This is not a triumphalist reframing. The information loss is real: the works' content is not in the tombstones; the textual recovery depends on the depositor's external backups and the recovery-archive's reconstruction. But the structural point — that suppression generates its own paratextual record, and that the record participates in the canonical history of the work it suppresses — is recognizable from the historical record of canon formation and applies to the present case.

## §16 LIMITATIONS

This document and the research program it describes are subject to substantial limitations that should be acknowledged independently of the falsifiability conditions stated in §12. Falsifiability concerns what would defeat the hypotheses if observed. Limitations concern what constrains the present state of evidence and inference, regardless of whether the hypotheses ultimately prevail.

*Incomplete corpus recovery.* The reconstructed DOI inventory of approximately 1,779 entries / 1,024 unique titled works is provisional and depends on external sources (DataCite, OpenAIRE, OpenAlex, the depositor's pre-termination backup). The full canonical record exists in Zenodo's internal systems and has not been disclosed. The attribution-severance phenomenon documented in the AXN:01 audit means that the inventory cannot, by construction, enumerate DOIs whose creator-metadata has been detached in ways the available reconstruction methods cannot trace.

*Unstable and personalized model behavior.* Many claims of machine recognition are based on observed model outputs that may be session-specific, personalized to the depositor's accounts, or affected by training-data contamination from the archive's own surfaces. Reproducibility across fresh sessions and across users without prior exposure to the archive is the standard against which such observations should ultimately be evaluated; the present documentary record does not consistently meet this standard.

*Unknown training exposure.* The degree to which contemporary language models were trained on CHA materials directly is not knowable from outside. Apparent recognition events may, in some unknown proportion, reflect direct training exposure rather than the corpus-level structural recognition the research program theorizes. Distinguishing these mechanisms requires access to training-data records that none of the relevant providers makes available.

*Inability to inspect commercial retrieval systems.* Search systems, AI Overviews, and retrieval surfaces are opaque to external audit. The Surface Visibility Instrument measures observable outputs; the underlying mechanisms producing those outputs are not directly observable.

*Changing search indexes.* Observations recorded at one moment may not be reproducible at a later moment as platforms change their indexes, retrieval algorithms, and content policies. The Capture Registry's value as a longitudinal record depends on its capture timestamps; later replication of recorded observations may not be possible.

*Automated traffic affecting download measures.* Download counts and other quantitative engagement measures are partially affected by automated traffic (web scrapers, training-data harvesters, archival systems) whose volume cannot be reliably distinguished from human-reader engagement.

*Observer-intervention reflexivity.* The author is simultaneously the experimenter and an object of study within the experiment. The author's interventions in retrieval surfaces (deposits made specifically to test or correct recognition behaviors) may alter the very phenomenon being observed. The research program acknowledges this reflexivity and treats it as part of the method; it remains a methodological constraint.

*Uneven preservation across the archive.* Some deposits are extensively preserved (with external sidecars, captures, and provenance documentation); others are preserved only by their DataCite metadata records. The evidence supporting different claims is therefore uneven, and the strongest evidence concentrates on later and more recent work.

*Lack of an external control corpus.* The strongest test of the machine-recognition claims would compare CHA recognition behavior to recognition of a size-matched, randomly assembled heterogeneous corpus without internal cross-references. This comparison has not been conducted. Until it is, the corpus-coherence claim remains methodologically incomplete.

*Author's dual role.* The author is the originating researcher, the corpus's author, the curator of the heteronymic system, the manager of the deposited materials, and the interpreter of the observed reception. This concentration of roles is methodologically defensible for practice-based research; it is also a substantial limitation on the independence of observation and interpretation. External replication and independent observation are the long-term remedies. The Assembly Chorus consultation is an interim partial remedy at the document-review layer; it is not a substitute for external replication of the archive's experimental conditions.

These limitations are not insulating qualifications; they identify the present state of the evidence honestly and locate the remaining work. The research program's hypotheses remain testable, and the limitations identify the conditions under which the strongest testing would become possible.

## §17 THE STRONGEST FORMULATION

The Crimson Hexagonal Archive was the operational capstone of the research program first formalized in the author's 2013 doctoral dissertation: a longitudinal experiment in deliberate synthetic canon formation. Its heterogeneous genres, institutions, heteronyms, metadata systems, citation networks, persistent identifiers, and machine-facing surfaces constituted the apparatus through which the research question was tested. The project asked whether a canon could be intentionally composed as a densely interreferential field whose parts progressively taught readers — both human and machine — how to recognize the whole. AI systems served as instruments, readers, reception environments, and objects of study within a research design originated, directed, evaluated, corrected, and documented by Lee Sharks. The archive's local heterogeneity was not evidence of an absent research basis; it was the experimental variable. Its emerging machine-mediated recognition, its institutional substrate failure, and its attempted regeneration after institutional severance constitute the observable longitudinal results of the program. The sovereign successor archive is at https://alexanarch.org. The research continues.
